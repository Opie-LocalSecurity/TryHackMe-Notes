# SOC Role in Blue Team

## Why “Hierarchy” Matters

- Security priorities differ by industry (e.g., law = confidentiality, factories = availability, hospitals = safety), so org charts and processes vary.
- Executives set business goals; a CISO (or equivalent) translates those goals into security strategy and builds specialized teams.

## Core Security Departments (large org focus)

- **Red Team**: Offensive testing to find weaknesses.
- **GRC**: Policies, risk management, and regulatory compliance (e.g., PCI DSS).
- **Blue Team**: Defense and monitoring (SOC-centric), incident response, engineering.

## Blue Team Overview

- Mission: Detect, investigate, and respond to attacks; scope and headcount vary widely (≈3–50 people).
- Sub-functions commonly include SOC, CIRT/CSIRT, and specialized roles.

## SOC (Security Operations Center)

- **Purpose**: First line of defense; triages alerts, investigates, tunes detections, coordinates with IT.
- **Typical roles**
    - **L1 Analyst**: Initial triage; escalate complex cases.
    - **L2 Analyst**: Deep investigations; handle advanced threats.
    - **Engineers**: Build/maintain tooling (e.g., EDR, SIEM); create detections.
    - **Manager**: Team operations, priorities, metrics.
- **Tooling focus**: SIEM for log aggregation/correlation, EDR for endpoint visibility/response, plus case management and SOAR.

## CIRT / CSIRT / CERT

- **When engaged**: High-severity or out-of-control incidents; operates with or beyond SOC toolset.
- **Capabilities**: Forensics, malware analysis, threat hunting, threat intel; high tempo and responsibility.
- **Examples**: National (e.g., JPCERT), private incident responders (e.g., Mandiant), cloud provider IR teams (e.g., AWS CIRT).

## Specialized Defensive Roles (common in large orgs)

- **Digital Forensics Analyst**: Disk/memory forensics; evidence handling.
- **Threat Intelligence Analyst**: Track actors, TTPs, IOCs; enrich detections.
- **AppSec Engineer**: Secure SDLC, code reviews, SAST/DAST, developer enablement.
- **AI Security/Research**: Study AI-driven threats and defenses.
- (Others often adjacent: Detection Engineering, Vulnerability Management, Cloud Security, Identity Security.)

## SOC Career Path & Getting Started

1. Build core SOC skills (log analysis, networking, Linux/Windows, EDR, SIEM); adjacent skills (red team, general IT) help.
2. Be proactive: CTFs, follow threat news, consider entry certifications (e.g., SAL1).
3. Prepare for interviews; understand differences between **internal SOC** and **MSSP** models.
4. After L1 experience, progress to L2 or pivot (engineering, CIRT, management).

## Internal SOC vs. MSSP (practical contrasts)

- **Scope**: Internal = protect one org; MSSP = many clients.
- **Pace**: Internal = steadier; MSSP = high alert volume and urgency.
- **Tools**: Internal = fewer, deeper expertise; MSSP = many, broad familiarity.
- **Exposure**: Internal = fewer major incidents; MSSP = frequent varied incidents (faster learning curve).

## Early-Career “Operating Principles”

- **Learn from every alert**: Document patterns, refine runbooks/detections.
- **Think like an attacker**: Map to kill chains/TTPs (MITRE ATT&CK).
- **Verify everything**: Validate signals, corroborate with multiple sources.
- **Get involved in incidents**: Volunteer for IR tasks; post-incident reviews accelerate growth.

## **Outcome to remember:**

Your first SOC role builds breadth—tooling, telemetry, and attacker mindset—so you can either specialize (DFIR, Threat Intel, AppSec, Detection Eng.) or progress to senior analyst/leadership (and eventually CISO) based on what resonates during real incidents.
