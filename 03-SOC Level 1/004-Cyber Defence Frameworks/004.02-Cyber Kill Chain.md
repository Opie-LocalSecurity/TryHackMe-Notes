# Cyber Kill Chain

## **Overview**

- The **Cyber Kill Chain®**, developed by **Lockheed Martin (2011)**, adapts a military attack model to describe the steps adversaries take during a cyberattack.
- It helps cybersecurity professionals—such as **SOC analysts, threat hunters, and incident responders**—understand, detect, and mitigate attacks at each phase.
- Understanding the chain enables better defense against **ransomware, APTs, and security breaches**, by identifying and closing security gaps.

## **Phases of the Cyber Kill Chain**

### **1. Reconnaissance**

- **Goal:** Gather intelligence on the target (systems, employees, technologies).
- **Methods:**
    - **Passive Recon:** No direct interaction (WHOIS, breach data, social media scraping).
    - **Active Recon:** Direct engagement (social engineering, port scanning, banner grabbing).
- **Key Concept:** **OSINT (Open-Source Intelligence)** – collecting public data from search engines, media, forums, databases, and WHOIS records.
- **Common Tools:** `theHarvester`, `Hunter.io`, `OSINT Framework`.
- **Example Activity:** **Email harvesting** for phishing campaigns.

### **2. Weaponization**

- **Goal:** Convert gathered data into attack tools (malware, exploits, payloads).
- **Key Terms:**
    - **Malware:** Malicious software to damage or gain access.
    - **Exploit:** Code leveraging vulnerabilities.
    - **Payload:** Code executed on the victim’s system.
- **Attacker Actions:**
    - Crafting malicious documents (e.g., macro-infected Office files).
    - Creating or buying malware (e.g., from the **Dark Web**).
    - Building C2 infrastructure or backdoors.
    - Designing believable **phishing templates** or fake OAuth apps.

### **3. Delivery**

- **Goal:** Transmit the payload to the target.
- **Common Vectors:**
    - **Phishing emails** (links or attachments).
    - **USB drops** (infected physical media).
    - **Watering hole attacks** (compromising legitimate websites for drive-by downloads).

### **4. Exploitation**

- **Goal:** Execute the malicious code on the target.
- **Techniques:**
    - **Malicious macro execution** via phishing.
    - **Zero-day exploits** or **known CVEs**.
- **Indicators of Compromise:**
    - Unexpected processes, registry changes, new services, or suspicious logs.
- May involve **privilege escalation** or **lateral movement** post-access.

### **5. Installation**

- **Goal:** Establish persistence and maintain access.
- **Techniques:**
    - Installing **web shells** (malicious PHP/ASP scripts).
    - Deploying **backdoors** (e.g., `Meterpreter`).
    - Modifying **Windows services** (MITRE technique `T1543.003`).
    - Using **Run keys / Startup folders** for persistence (`T1547.001`).
    - Applying **Timestomping (`T1070.006`)** to evade detection.
- Enables attacker reentry even after system cleanup or patching.

### **6. Command & Control (C2)**

- **Goal:** Establish remote communication with the compromised system.
- **Mechanism:** The infected host “beacons” to the attacker’s C2 server for instructions.
- **Common Channels:**
    - **HTTP/HTTPS** (port 80/443) to blend in with normal traffic.
    - **DNS tunneling** (data exfiltration via DNS queries).
- Attackers may operate their own or hijacked C2 infrastructure.

### **7. Actions on Objectives (Exfiltration)**

- **Goal:** Achieve the attacker’s intent (data theft, damage, or disruption).
- **Typical Actions:**
    - Credential harvesting and **privilege escalation**.
    - **Internal reconnaissance** and **lateral movement**.
    - **Data exfiltration**, **deletion of backups**, or **data corruption**.

## **Limitations & Modern Context**

- The **original Kill Chain (2011)** focuses on **perimeter defense** and **malware-based attacks**.
- It lacks coverage for **insider threats** and modern **multi-vector TTPs** (tactics, techniques, procedures).
- Modern defense should also integrate:
    - **MITRE ATT&CK** – detailed adversary behavior mapping.
    - **Unified Kill Chain** – expanded, modernized model combining multiple frameworks.

## ✅ **Key Takeaway:**

The Cyber Kill Chain provides a structured view of cyberattacks, allowing defenders to detect and disrupt adversaries early in their operations—but it should be supplemented with modern frameworks (like MITRE ATT&CK and Unified Kill Chain) for comprehensive defense.
